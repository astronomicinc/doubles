We built Doubles because we got tired of dating apps. This is the story behind why we started, and where we're headed.

Why we started

We got tired of apps and algorithms.

We're founders who understand what it's like to be selective about who you spend time with. Dating apps broke something real.

The problem with swiping

If you're successful—if you've built something, raised capital, or led a team—dating apps feel insulting. You're comparing humans like startups, reducing someone to five photos and a bio. The algorithm is supposed to be smart, but it's optimizing for engagement, not compatibility. You get flooded with messages from people who don't know you and never will.

The irony is that as a founder, you understand the power of networks and human judgment. You'd never make a hire based on an algorithm alone. You'd want to know who introduced them, what they've built, what they care about. Yet somehow dating apps convinced us to ignore that wisdom when it comes to meeting partners.

Why friend-vetted is better

Your friend knows you. They know what matters to you, how you show up, what you're looking for. When they introduce you to someone they'd vouch for, there's credibility in that introduction. It's not an algorithm guessing; it's someone you trust betting on a connection.

That's the whole point of the bring-a-friend format. You show up with someone who knows you, who can vouch for you. That person also brings a friend. Suddenly you're in a room with 30 people who all came with intention, all brought by someone, all vetted by someone you respect. The quality shifts immediately.

Where we're headed

We're building the default way founders meet.

Our five-year vision is ambitious: to become the way successful people in tech meet each other romantically in San Francisco. Not an app. Not a novelty. An institution.

From SF to the world

We're starting in San Francisco because this is where the density of ambitious founders and investors is highest. We'll prove the model works here, build a reputation, and then expand to other cities: New York, Austin, Los Angeles. Eventually, London and Singapore.

But this will never be a franchise. Each city will have its own identity, hosted in the homes of its most successful people, curated by people who know their community. The rigor and taste will scale. The algorithm will never show up.

Building for real people

We're not trying to maximize engagement or sell data. We're trying to build something that actually works for the people we serve. That means we'll always cap our events at 30 people. We'll always require vetting. We'll always keep out the flakes.

Success for us isn't a metric. It's a breakup that turns into a marriage. It's a business partnership that started over drinks. It's friendships that lasted longer than the dates. Those are the stories we want to hear.
