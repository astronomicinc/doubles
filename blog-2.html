Building Community for the Ambitious

May 20, 2026 · 6 min read
← Back to Blog

Dating is a network effect. The better the people in the room, the better the outcomes for everyone.

This isn't controversial. We've known for years that matchmaking is about curation. The luxury matchmakers (Raya, etc.) charge $10K+ because they've curated a genuinely elite group. The constraint creates value. Vetting is the feature.

But Raya is expensive, transactional, and one-on-one. It's not community. There's no feedback loop. You don't know the other people in the pool. You're just matched alone.

What if you built the opposite: hyper-curated, group-based events where everyone is vetted, everyone knows the vetting came from someone they trust, and the whole community is intentional?

The Power of the Bring-a-Friend Model

The mechanism is simple: To attend a Doubles event, you bring someone you'd vouch for. That person also brings someone. Everyone at the event was brought by someone.

This creates three things at once:

  • Vetting by humans, not algorithms. Your friend knows whether you're a good person, whether you're serious about dating, whether you're someone they'd want in their network.
  • Reputation accountability. When you vouch for someone, your reputation is on the line. You're not bringing your terrible ex or someone who's going to cause drama. You're bringing people you actually stand behind.
  • Automatic gender balance. Men bring women. Women bring men. You don't end up with 70 men and 10 women. Balance is inherent to the model.

This is radically different from apps where you swipe alone, or singles events where anyone with $45 can show up.

Why Community Matters

When you're a founder or executive, you live in networks. You know your co-founders, your investors, your advisors, your team. Dating should work the same way. Not through cold algorithms, but through people you trust.

Communities also create repeat engagement. You meet someone at an event, it doesn't work out, but you see them at the next event. No awkwardness, just "hey, good to see you." You also meet their friends and make new connections. The community grows organically.

The strongest communities are built on intentionality. Everyone chose to show up. Everyone brought someone. Everyone is in the same life stage (post-exit founder, $200K+ executive, investor). Shared context is a foundation.

The Venue Matters Too

Doubles events happen in the private homes of founders and investors. This is deliberate.

When you're in someone's home, the energy is different. It's intimate, not transactional. You're in a space that reflects their taste, their success, their story. The conversation is deeper because you're not performing for a room full of strangers—you're in someone's private space, which means respect and authenticity are implicit.

Plus, the story is better. "I met them at a dating event at a Agi House in Twin Peaks with Golden Gate Bridge views" beats "I met them at a restaurant in SOMA."

Intentionality Over Convenience

Apps are convenient. You swipe while you're on the couch. Convenience attracts massive volume, which dilutes quality.

Doubles events require intention. You have to apply. You have to be approved. You have to bring a friend. You have to show up on a specific Saturday evening.

This friction is a feature, not a bug. The people who show up actually want to be there. They've done the work of finding someone to bring. They've invested the time. The intent is unmistakable.

Building Something Real

The vision isn't just events. It's infrastructure for ambitious people to meet. Eventually, it's a community where the default way successful people date is through Doubles, not apps.

That happens because:

  • The person you date is vettable (you can ask "how do you know them?")
  • The venue is aspirational (you want to tell people where you met)
  • The community grows through word-of-mouth (your friends know other people who'd be great)
  • The experience is intentional (everyone's here for the same reason, at the same caliber)

This is the kind of infrastructure that's hard to replicate. Apps can be cloned. Events with curated homes and founder network moats? Much harder.

That's why it works.
